**Figure 5. Angle-origin G-AZ coverage and the separation-domain principle.**

**(A)** Empirical event-count distributions for Ascendant azimuth (Asc G-AZ, blue) and Equatorial Ascendant azimuth (EA G-AZ, orange) across all 57,635 events, binned at 1° resolution. Both angle origins occupy bounded azimuthal arcs (eastern horizon sector in this dataset): Asc spans 53°–126° (74° total; 72/360 occupied bins), and EA spans 68°–112° (45° total; 45/360 occupied bins). Within these arcs, sampling is dense (maximum gap between consecutive occupied bins ≤ 3°), but the majority of the 0–360° domain remains unoccupied. Dashed and dotted vertical lines indicate the empirical bounds for Asc and EA respectively. Midheaven (MC) and Vertex (VX) azimuth values are not generated in the Swiss Ephemeris pipeline and are therefore not shown (see Methods §2.2).

**(B)** Empirical angular-separation distribution for Asc G-AZ × Jupiter G-AZ across all events, binned at 1° resolution. Despite the bounded origin-domain coverage shown in Panel A, the separation distribution spans the full 0–360° domain. This demonstrates the separation-domain principle: angular-separation features are circular differences computed modulo 360°, and the domain of separation is not constrained by the domain of the origin coordinate when the paired target achieves effective full-circle sampling through aggregate observation under Earth's rotation. The observed bimodal structure, with peaks near 0° (conjunction) and 180° (opposition), reflects the geometric pairing of a bounded-arc origin with a full-circle target distribution rather than directional asymmetry in the underlying relationship. Panel B operationalizes the local (concentration) signal regime defined in Methods §2.2.
